CAD CAM Software

It also has broader capabilities including basic mesh modelling, 3+2 routing, structural analysis, CFD at a limited level, plate metal parts, and other features.

It is written in Java and is available from our website for macOS, Windows, and Linux. The design tools are free. The CAM features are sold with a perpetual license that includes one year of upgrades.

I am not currently planning to run ads for the software. My current thinking is that the strongest use of the software is as part of the product ecosystem around the notcher and bender.


Kit Car Program

The kit car was the original inspiration for the company, but building cars directly is beyond my risk tolerance for this year. I have already spent more money than I am comfortable with and need to prioritize revenue generation.

The car itself is a mid-engine, two-seat sports car built around a steel tube frame chassis and a composite body. It uses a custom glass windshield because no OEM glass is available for the design. The glass was about $5,000 many years ago and is important because plastic is not durable enough for a practical road car.

The chassis is steel, which makes it workable by a skilled builder without specialized tools. Repairs and modifications are possible. The chassis is also modular, with a front bumper section, occupant cell, and rear bumper so parts can be replaced more easily.

Building a car remains a major undertaking with many parts, safety concerns, and registration requirements.


Kit Car Registration

In British Columbia, the current registration path for a kit car appears to be:

1. Contact ICBC
2. Send the vehicle to a VCSE inspection facility for a safety inspection
3. Drive to a weigh scale to get vehicle weight
4. Have ICBC mail the VIN tag to the inspection facility for application
5. Insure the vehicle after the process is complete

The declaration form appears to treat the builder as selling the vehicle to the builder.

This pathway is useful, but I should treat regulatory and liability issues carefully if I enable others to build vehicles from my plans.

Body Panels and Moulds

The prototype body was made from fiberglass because it is easier to work with using a layup process. Fiberglass folds around edges better than carbon, but it is heavier.

Carbon parts are harder to make because they often require vacuum bagging, getting an airtight seal can take hours, and failure can mean parts need to be discarded. Adjustments are also harder once the process is underway.

The current production plan is to document instructions for builders using our moulds and a hybrid layup approach with carbon fiber veil, carbon weave on flatter sections, and fiberglass on bends. Either layup or infusion may be used depending on what works best in practice.

Right now the expectation is that we would help builder partners fabricate panels. I may also make a few panels on request.


5-Axis Router for Body Moulds

We built a custom 5-axis router to cut foam moulds for the kit car. We ordered 2 lb foam blocks in 4' x 8' sizes at 12" and 16" thickness. The foam blocks are about $300 each, which is a good material cost for this purpose.

The router uses our CAD CAM software and is good enough for our own mould-making needs. While we also sell the software, I am not sure we can compete broadly with tools like Fusion 360 in the 5-axis routing market.

One reason we built our own systems instead of buying or outsourcing was to create a foundation we could extend. We had considered automating panel manufacturing with the router, but I am not focused on that anymore.

Right now the router is mainly valuable because it gives us the ability to make the moulds we need ourselves. A major risk is that the router is large and complex. If it fails while I still need it, I could lose time to repair and recalibration.

After the body panels are made, I may disassemble the router. I could also offer routing services to others, but I am not currently convinced that this scales well or is worth my attention.

I previously ran a small ad campaign and got one interested person who did not have the skills to design the required parts. That result did not make me want to pursue routing services further.

2. What the minimum shippable notcher package includes
3. How the first ten customers will actually be found and closed
4. What post-sale support will cost in time and money
5. Whether the vehicle licensing model is worth the legal and support complexity
6. How much founder time should go to kit car work versus machine sales


Open Questions and Notes

This section is for things I do not fully know yet but that may matter materially.

Break-Even and Cash Flow

1. Is $16,000 CAD per month the correct break-even number once all real recurring costs are included?
2. What is the true all-in cost per notcher after warranty risk, shipping preparation, support time, and failed builds are included?
3. How many notchers need to be sold per month, quarter, and year to create enough margin to stabilize the business?

Sales Process

1. Which buyer segment is most likely to buy first: universities, fab shops, builders, or retailers?
2. What are the top reasons a serious buyer would delay or reject a purchase?
3. Is an introductory offer worth it, or does it risk setting the wrong anchor price?
4. How many direct outreach conversations are needed to produce one sale?

Productization

1. What exactly must be ready before I can confidently ship the first units?
2. What parts of assembly, calibration, and setup are still too dependent on me personally?
3. What documentation is required so a buyer can succeed without excessive support?

Operations

1. What work should the current employee own?
2. What work can the co-op student realistically help with during the summer?
3. What is the bottleneck in production: assembly time, parts sourcing, testing, documentation, or sales?

Vehicle Program

1. What legal structure and contract terms would be required for a builder licensing model?
2. How much support would builders realistically need from me?
3. Is the vehicle program helping the business this year, or mainly competing for time and attention?


Current Priority Rule

For this year, the decision rule should be simple:

If the work does not directly help sell, ship, support, or improve the CNC tube notcher, it should usually wait.
